James Frank Sacco

**Introduction**

Andrej Karpathy coined the term "vibe coding," and now whole industries are using it or plan to use it. How many of these tech leaders and CEOs know that Karpathy basically entirely hand-wrote the entire tool he named Nanochat?  He never meant for vive coding to replace human software developers. According to him the LLM was "not too bad for throwaway weekend projects." <sup>1</sup> 

When the software you build goes into making drugs for cancer patients, how do you maintain integrity and safety in bioinformatics? How do you resist investor, market, and leadership pressure to adopt AI tools that are more trouble than they’re worth. 

When I took my Intro to Genomic Data Science Specialization on Coursera, the professor started out with a cautionary tale of Duke researchers who got sued because of clinical trials that began based on faulty research that was published.

With the advent of vibe coding, I anticipate it is only a matter of time before a major healthcare system or drug company comes after some poor bioinformatician whose work got published in a big journal based on a vibe coded finding.

This is the motivation for my effort here.

The aim of this effort is to survey and stay up to date on new insights in AI skepticism broadly: issues in measurement, integrity, provenance, sampling, and coding. To narrow such a vast trove of information, I will try to stick to concerns encountered by bioinformaticians who work on biomedical data. I will not cover metagenomics, evolution, biochemistry, proteomics, structural biology, chemical biology, or ecology. Instead, the main focus will be on issues relevant to genomic, epigenomic, and transcriptomic data analysis. 

The eventual result will be a robust work to consult as a warning sign before using the latest new AI tool or frontier model. In other words, I will “blunt the leading edge” to borrow the motto of The Baffler magazine.

Many of the notes will come from AI experts, bioinformaticians, professors, and thought leaders I happen to find on my social media feeds or from papers I find interesting.
